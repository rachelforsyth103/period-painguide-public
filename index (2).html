Other symptoms worth flagging

Pain before your period starts

Cramping that begins more than 1–2 days before flow can signal endometriosis or adenomyosis — even at moderate intensity.

Pain during sex, urination, or bowel movements

Pelvic pain in any of these contexts — especially around your cycle — is one of the most commonly dismissed endometriosis symptoms.

OTC medication doesn't work

Standard doses of ibuprofen or naproxen should control typical period pain. Needing more, or finding them ineffective, is a clinical signal.

Heavy bleeding alongside pain

Soaking a pad or tampon every 1–2 hours, combined with significant pain, can indicate fibroids, adenomyosis, or related conditions.

Missing work, school, or activities

Losing 1 or more days per month to period pain — regardless of your pain score — is clinically significant and warrants evaluation.

Pain that's getting worse over time

Dysmenorrhea that progressively worsens across cycles, rather than staying stable, is a pattern associated with endometriosis.

Why this tool exists: Research shows that after symptoms begin, patients wait 1–4 years before seeking medical attention — and the two biggest reasons are not knowing the symptoms are abnormal, and having been told the pain is just part of having a period. This tool is designed to directly address both. This is a reference, not a diagnosis. Pain tolerance varies — if something feels wrong for your body, that's enough reason to bring it up with a provider.
